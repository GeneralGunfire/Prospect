Discussion
Platform vs. Problems — Does the Design Address the Research?

The research identified a clear problem: South African high school learners make subject and career decisions without adequate, consolidated information. Hooley et al. (2013) established that structured career guidance measurably improves educational outcomes, but South Africa's guidance counsellor shortage — roughly one counsellor per 800 learners in many public schools (Mncwango, 2016) — means most students never access formal guidance at all. [1][2]

Prospect's career explorer, quiz, and roadmap directly address this gap. The platform offers what a counsellor would typically provide: salary ranges by sector, subject consequences, provincial job demand, and a clear path from the learner's current grade to a career goal — for 400 careers, available at any time, at no cost.

The research also found that TVET colleges are consistently misrepresented in guidance materials, with the DHET White Paper (2013) and Wedekind (2010) both documenting how vocational routes are presented as fallback options rather than first choices. [3][4] The TVET section in Prospect was built specifically to counter this, presenting TVET colleges alongside universities with equal prominence and detail.

On the infrastructure side, Eberhard et al. (2014) and Zondo and Mncube (2022) documented the direct link between load shedding and academic disruption. [11][12] The load shedding tracker and water dashboard address this by giving students visibility into disruptions before they happen, so study sessions can be planned around them rather than lost to them.

Feasibility — Time, Labour, Finance, and Scale

Feasibility analysis asks whether the project was achievable in the way it was attempted, and whether it can grow from here. The platform was built by a single developer over 55 days. That fact defines both what is impressive about what was achieved and where the natural constraints are.

Time
55 Days — Realistic but Tight
The core platform — career explorer, bursary finder, AI assistant, and community features — was achievable in 55 days because the data was sourced rather than generated, and the tech stack (React, Supabase, Groq) has mature tooling. The study library is where time ran out: properly mapping all NSC subjects with structured, accurate topic notes is weeks of content work alone. One week per subject, 11 subjects = 11 weeks of content at minimum. That is not 55 days.
Labour
Solo Build — Sustainable to a Point
A single developer handled architecture, data curation, UI, testing, and deployment. This is feasible for a prototype and first release. For the library to scale to full NSC coverage, it requires either a content team — educators or subject specialists — or a structured data pipeline. TypeScript type-safety on the career and bursary data files kept errors low, but hand-curating 400 career profiles is still a significant labour investment that would be difficult to sustain solo for an expanded library.
Finance
Currently Zero-Cost — Fragile at Scale
The platform runs at zero direct cost using Vercel free tier, Supabase free tier, and Groq free tier. This is genuinely impressive for a student project. The risk is that free tiers have limits: Groq's rate limits will constrain concurrent users, Supabase's free database rows cap at 50,000, and Vercel's bandwidth cap applies. A platform handling thousands of active daily users would need paid hosting — approximately R1,500–R4,000/month depending on traffic — which requires either sponsorship, a grant, or institutional backing.

Significance and Value

The significance of Prospect is not in being the only platform that tries to help South African learners — it is in being the only one that tries to address all of the relevant problems in one place, for free, on mobile, without requiring registration to access the most useful parts.

Traxler (2018) argues that mobile-first design is not a preference in developing-world education contexts — it is the only architecture that reaches the majority of students, since smartphone ownership vastly outpaces laptop or desktop access among SA high schoolers. [6] Prospect was designed mobile-first from week one, with a data saver mode and sub-2.5s load time to serve students on limited prepaid data.

Holmes et al. (2019) note that AI in education tools often fail because they are generic — not calibrated to the specific curriculum, grading system, or institutional context students are working within. [8] The AI School Assistant addresses this directly: the Groq system prompt is focused on South African education — NSC requirements, APS scoring, TVET pathways, and SA university admission — rather than being a general-purpose chatbot with SA context bolted on.

Limitations and Errors

A research project that doesn't state its limitations plainly is not being honest about what it found. The following limitations are real constraints on what Prospect can currently do — not hypothetical risks, but gaps that exist in the launched platform. Some are fixable with time and resources. Some are structural.

Study Library — Incomplete Content Coverage Critical
The study library covers 24 topics across 11 subjects. The NSC curriculum for a Grade 10–12 learner spans far more: each subject has multiple terms, each term has multiple topics, and each topic needs to be accurately mapped to the curriculum, not approximated. Building 24 topics took a significant portion of the project timeline. Full NSC coverage, done properly, is months of content work that one developer cannot do in 55 days. The current library is a working proof-of-concept, not a complete study tool. Students should know this, and it is not hidden in the interface.
Groq API — Rate Limit and Third-Party Dependency Critical
The AI School Assistant depends entirely on Groq's free-tier API. Groq can change its pricing, reduce its free tier, or experience downtime — any of which would take the AI feature offline. The platform has no fallback if Groq becomes unavailable. Additionally, the free-tier rate limit means the AI feature could degrade under high concurrent load. The 7 predefined instant answers reduce API calls for common questions, but a fully AI-driven session will hit the free tier ceiling if enough users are active simultaneously. This is not a design flaw — it is the honest cost of building an AI feature on free infrastructure.
Financial Sustainability — Zero Cost, Zero Safety Net Moderate
The platform currently costs nothing to run because Vercel, Supabase, and Groq all have free tiers adequate for low-traffic use. If Prospect were to grow — which is the point — those free tiers would be exhausted. Vercel's bandwidth limits, Supabase's row limits, and Groq's request limits all cap at levels that a genuinely popular educational platform would exceed. There is no revenue model, no institutional backing, and no grant. This is fine for a student expo project; it is a real constraint for a production service used by thousands of students daily.
Data Currency — Career and Bursary Data Will Age Moderate
The 400 career profiles and 245 bursary listings were sourced and verified at the time of development (March–May 2026). Salary figures change. Bursaries open and close. Universities change their minimum APS requirements. Without a systematic update process — either manual or via scrapers — this data will become inaccurate over time. The load shedding and water dashboard data is kept live via automated scrapers, but the career and bursary data is static TypeScript files that need manual updates.
AI Accuracy — LLM Hallucination Risk Moderate
Groq's llama-3.3-70b-versatile model is accurate on general education questions but is not infallible. Large language models can produce plausible-sounding but incorrect answers, especially for specific factual claims — exact APS requirements, specific university cut-off scores, bursary application deadlines. The system prompt is designed to focus the model on SA education and encourage it to recommend the platform's data as a source of record rather than generating figures from inference. But this is a prompt-based mitigation, not a technical guarantee. Students using the AI should be aware it can be wrong on specifics.

Conclusion
The goal was achieved.

The research question was whether a single, free digital platform could meaningfully close the information gap South African high school learners face when making subject and career decisions. The answer, based on what was built and verified at launch, is yes — with an important qualification.

Prospect launched on 15 May 2026 with 400 career profiles, 245 bursary listings, a working AI school assistant, live infrastructure dashboards, and a study library covering 11 subjects. All 31 end-to-end tests passed. All 8 engineering goals were met. The platform is free to access, mobile-first, and live on Vercel.

The qualification is the study library. The infrastructure is correct, the architecture is sound, and the first 24 topics prove the system works. But 24 topics is not comprehensive curriculum coverage. A student studying for matric needs far more than what is currently there. The platform is a foundation — what it can grow into is more significant than what it is right now. The core problems identified in the research are addressed. The platform works. The goal was achieved.
