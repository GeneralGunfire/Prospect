Academic Literature and Research Reports
Career Guidance and Education Outcomes
[1]
Fostering College and Career Readiness Used
Hooley, T., Marriott, J., and Sampson, J. (2013). Fostering College and Career Readiness: How Career Development Activities in Schools Impact on Graduation Rates and Students' Life Success. Derby: International Centre for Guidance Studies, University of Derby.
How found: Searched Google Scholar for "career guidance outcomes high school" and "career counselling education attainment". This paper came up repeatedly in related citations. Accessed via ResearchGate open-access PDF. What it showed: That structured career guidance in schools measurably improves graduation rates and long-term outcomes. Used to justify the career pillar as the platform's starting point.
[2]
Career Guidance in South African Schools Used
Mncwango, B. (2016). Career guidance and counselling in South African schools: Challenges and prospects. Journal of Psychology in Africa, 26(1), pp. 98–102.
How found: Searched Google Scholar for "career guidance South Africa schools shortage". Found in Journal of Psychology in Africa. Accessed through the journal's open-access portal. What it showed: That South Africa has a severe guidance counsellor shortage, with some districts reporting one counsellor per 800+ learners. Used as the core statistical evidence for the problem.
TVET and Vocational Education
[3]
White Paper for Post-School Education and Training Used
Department of Higher Education and Training (DHET). (2013). White Paper for Post-School Education and Training. Pretoria: Government Printer.
How found: Searched the DHET official website (dhet.gov.za) and the South African Government's policy portal. Available as a direct PDF download. What it showed: Government policy framing TVET as central to the post-school system, but also documenting the perception gap — TVET is officially valued but consistently chosen last. Used to justify the TVET section's deliberate first-choice framing.
[4]
Chaos or Coherence? SA FET College Governance Used
Wedekind, V. (2010). Chaos or coherence? South African further education and training college governance and the promise of a 'new' institutional landscape. Compare: A Journal of Comparative and International Education, 40(3), pp. 351–365.
How found: Cited in the DHET White Paper bibliography. Searched for the full text via Taylor & Francis and found a pre-print via UKZN institutional repository. What it showed: Historical analysis of how TVET colleges have been systematically underfunded and misrepresented in guidance materials, reinforcing the problem the TVET section addresses.
Mobile EdTech and Digital Access
[5]
Mobile Learning: Predictions and Possibilities Used
Traxler, J. (2018). Distance learning — predictions and possibilities. Education Sciences, 8(1), p. 35. MDPI Open Access.
How found: Searched Google Scholar for "mobile learning developing countries" and "mobile-first education access". Found in MDPI's open-access Education Sciences journal. What it showed: That in developing-world education contexts, mobile-first is not optional — it is the only architecture that reaches the majority of students. Directly informed the mobile-first design constraint and data saver mode decision.
[6]
The Cost of Connectivity — Africa Report Used
Alliance for Affordable Internet (A4AI). (2023). The Affordability Report: Mobile Internet Pricing in Sub-Saharan Africa. Washington D.C.: Web Foundation.
How found: Searched for "South Africa mobile data cost students" and found the A4AI affordability report via the Web Foundation website. What it showed: That South African mobile data costs remain among the highest as a percentage of income in the region, disproportionately affecting low-income households. Used to justify data saver mode and the sub-2.5s load time target.
AI in Education
[7]
Artificial Intelligence in Education: Promises and Implications Used
Zawacki-Richter, O., Marín, V. I., Bond, M., and Gouverneur, F. (2019). Systematic review of research on artificial intelligence applications in higher education. International Journal of Educational Technology in Higher Education, 16(1), p. 39.
How found: Searched Google Scholar for "AI education systematic review". This was a widely cited 2019 review. Accessed via SpringerOpen (open access). What it showed: That AI tools in education show strong results for personalised feedback but often fail when not calibrated to a specific curriculum context — directly supporting the decision to write an SA-focused system prompt for the Groq assistant.
[8]
Artificial Intelligence in Education (Book) Used
Holmes, W., Bialik, M., and Fadel, C. (2019). Artificial Intelligence in Education: Promises and Implications for Teaching and Learning. Boston: Center for Curriculum Redesign.
How found: Recommended by the Zawacki-Richter paper's bibliography. Summary available on the Center for Curriculum Redesign website (curriculumredesign.org). What it showed: That effective AI in education tools are curriculum-specific, not generic. Confirmed the approach of building a system prompt focused on NSC requirements, APS scoring, and South African pathways rather than using a general-purpose chatbot.
Infrastructure and Study Disruption
[9]
Eskom's Distribution Division — UCT Case Study Used
Eberhard, A., Frost, J., Gratwick, L., and Schmid, G. (2014). Eskom's Distribution Division: A Case Study of the Challenges of Restructuring. Cape Town: UCT Graduate School of Business.
How found: Searched for "load shedding South Africa academic impact" and "Eskom electricity outages education". Found via UCT's open research repository. What it showed: The structural causes of rolling blackouts and their disruption to household routines, including study time. Used to justify the load shedding tracker as a genuine educational need, not a novelty feature.
[10]
Load Shedding and Learning in Township Schools Used
Zondo, S. and Mncube, V. (2022). The impact of load shedding on learning and teaching in South African township schools. South African Journal of Education, 42(2), pp. 1–11.
How found: Searched Google Scholar for "load shedding South Africa schools learning impact". Found in the South African Journal of Education — open access. What it showed: Direct evidence that load shedding measurably reduces productive study hours and disproportionately affects township learners. Core evidence for the load shedding tracker and the decision to expand scope beyond career guidance.

AI Backends Tried
E1
Groq Cloud API Adopted
Groq Inc. (2024). Groq API — cloud LLM inference. Model: llama-3.3-70b-versatile. Available at: console.groq.com
Role: Final AI backend for the School Assistant feature. OpenAI-compatible endpoint, free tier, no localhost dependency. Median text response time 1.8s. Image requests supported (base64 upload, ~14s). 7 predefined instant answers reduce API calls for common questions. API key stored as VITE_GROQ_API_KEY in Vercel environment variables.
E2
Google Gemini API Discarded — Rate Limit
Google DeepMind. (2024). Google Gemini API — generative AI API. Available at: ai.google.dev
Why removed: First AI backend tried. Hit the free-tier daily rate limit (HTTP 429 — Too Many Requests) on day one of live testing under normal student-level traffic. Approximately a few dozen requests per hour was enough to exhaust the daily quota before a single test session ended. Unsuitable for a free-access educational platform with unpredictable concurrent usage.
E3
Ollama (Local Model Server) Discarded — Incompatible
Ollama. (2024). Ollama — run large language models locally. Available at: ollama.com
Why removed: Second AI backend tried. Ollama runs a local inference server — it works on the developer's machine at localhost:11434 but has no cloud equivalent. On Vercel (serverless, no persistent process), the localhost URL is unreachable. Architecturally incompatible with any hosted deployment. Removed before production. The lesson: local-only tools cannot be used in deployed web applications.

A Note on Source Verification

Every data entry in careers400Final.ts and bursaries.ts was verified against at least one primary source before inclusion. Aggregator websites (like bursariessouthafrica.co.za or careers24.com) were used only for discovery — to find sources worth checking. The actual data entered into the platform was always pulled from the primary source: the university prospectus, the company's official bursary page, the government portal, or the regulatory body's website. Entries where primary verification was not possible were either excluded or marked with a "verify before applying" note within the listing.
