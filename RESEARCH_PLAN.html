Section 1
01

Research Overview

Research for this project happened in three distinct phases that each served a different purpose. Before development started, research was about understanding the problem and the landscape — what information gap existed, what had already been tried, and what the platform needed to do that nothing else did. During development, research was practical and immediate — every time a decision had to be made about a tool, a feature, or an approach, the available options were investigated before committing. After the core platform was built, research shifted to verification — checking that the decisions made were the right ones, that features worked as intended, and that the platform actually addressed the problems it was built for.

This document covers all three phases in full. Each research activity is documented with what was investigated, what sources were used, what was found, and what decision it led to. Nothing in here is background reading for its own sake — every item of research changed or confirmed something about the platform.

Research at a Glance
  • 22 March 2026 — Pre-Development
    Problem and landscape research
    Competitor analysis of 4 existing SA platforms. Literature review of career guidance, TVET, edtech access, and infrastructure barriers. SA education statistics gathered. Bursary and career data sourced and verified.
  • 25 March – 14 May 2026 — During Development
    Technical and design decision research
    Framework evaluation (Next.js vs React/Vite). State management options. AI backend trials (Gemini, Ollama, Groq). Database and auth provider selection. Deployment platform comparison. Mobile data cost context. Load shedding impact research.
  • 15 May 2026 — Post-Development
    Verification and outcome research
    End-to-end testing across all user flows. Design criteria audit. Feature scope review against original aims. Performance measurement against defined targets. Data completeness verification.
Research approach: This project used a design-based research methodology — research informed design decisions, those decisions were implemented, and the results were evaluated against the original research findings. Where the evaluation showed a mismatch (a feature that did not meet a criterion, or a tool that did not work as expected), further research was done to find a better approach. This cycle ran continuously throughout the 55-day development period.
Section 2
02

Phase 1 — Research Before Development

Before a single line of code was written, four areas of research were completed. The purpose was to build a clear, evidence-based picture of the problem before deciding what to build. Skipping this step is one of the most common reasons edtech tools get built that no one uses — they solve the problem the developer imagined rather than the problem students actually have.

Research Area 1 — The South African Education Gap
Pre-Development · 22 March 2026
Career counsellor availability in South African schools
22 March 2026

Investigated how many South African schools have a dedicated, functioning career counsellor. Sources used: Department of Basic Education reports, the Mncwango (2016) study in Perspectives in Education, and media reporting on guidance teacher shortages.

Finding: the majority of South African schools — particularly those in rural and township areas — do not have a dedicated career counsellor. Where guidance does exist, it tends to focus on the university application process rather than subject-choice consequences or TVET options. For learners at under-resourced schools, the guidance gap is effectively complete.

Decision: The platform needed to function as a substitute for a career counsellor — not a supplement to one. This ruled out building a tool that assumed prior guidance existed.
APS system and subject-choice consequences
22 March 2026

Investigated how the APS (Admission Point Score) system works, how it is calculated, and which subject combinations were required for entry into different faculties at South African universities. Sources: DHET publications, individual university prospectuses (UCT, Wits, UP, UJ, UKZN, SU, UWC).

Finding: the consequences of subject choices made in Grade 10 are permanent and wide-ranging. Dropping Mathematics for Mathematical Literacy closes access to engineering, medicine, actuarial science, architecture, and most applied sciences. This is rarely communicated explicitly to students at the time of the decision.

Decision: Built the APS calculator and wired subject requirements directly into career profiles, so the link between today's subject choice and tomorrow's career access is visible at the same time.
TVET college enrollment vs perception
22 March 2026

Researched TVET college enrollment numbers, qualification outcomes, and graduate employment rates. Sources: DHET White Paper for Post-School Education and Training (2013), Stats SA education series, Wedekind (2010) research on TVET perception.

Finding: TVET colleges enroll more students than all 26 public universities combined, yet are consistently framed as a lesser option. The artisan skills shortage in South Africa is acute and well-documented — precisely the fields TVET trains for. The perception gap is a documented policy problem, not just a cultural assumption.

Decision: TVET would be built as a first-class section of Prospect with full navigational parity to university information. Design criteria included a TVET parity requirement that the section must never appear as secondary.
Research Area 2 — Competitor Analysis
Pre-Development · 22 March 2026
Existing South African edtech platforms — feature mapping
22 March 2026

Reviewed four platforms in the South African education guidance space: Yenza, Gradesmatch, NCAP, and Reslocate. Each was evaluated across six criteria: career information quality, whether data was SA-specific, bursary coverage, TVET focus, study resources, and life context (infrastructure, costs, civic).

Sources: direct platform review, app store listings, user reviews, and media coverage of each platform.

Finding: no existing platform addressed all six criteria. Career information existed across platforms but was rarely South African-specific — salary data, for example, was frequently sourced from international databases. No platform addressed TVET as a primary pathway. No platform provided study resources. No platform addressed the infrastructural context of South African student life.

Decision: Prospect would be built to fill the full gap — not to compete on any single feature but to be the only platform that covered all six areas together. This became the basis for the three-pillar structure.
Research Area 3 — Data Sources and Verification
Pre-Development · 22–25 March 2026
Bursary database — sourcing and verification
22–25 March 2026

South African bursary information was gathered from: university financial aid offices, National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) documentation, government department bursary pages (DBE, DHET, DSI, DMRE, DPW), state-owned enterprise bursary programs (Eskom, Transnet, Sasol, Anglo American, De Beers), and private company programs (Discovery, Standard Bank, FNB, Investec, Old Mutual).

Each entry was normalized to a consistent schema before being included. A BURSARY-REVIEW.md audit file was maintained tracking which entries had been verified and which had gaps. Entries with missing required fields were either completed through additional research or excluded.

Final count: 245 verified bursary entries.

Decision: Established the data schema and audit process that all future data additions would follow. An incomplete entry was treated as worse than no entry.
Career data — South African salary and demand research
22–21 April 2026

Career salary data was sourced from: PayScale South Africa, CareerJunction annual salary surveys, Careers24 job market reports, and sector-specific reports (South African Institution of Civil Engineering, Engineering Council of SA, SAICA for accounting). Provincial job demand data was sourced from Stats SA quarterly labour force surveys.

International salary databases (US Bureau of Labor Statistics, UK National Careers Service) were explicitly not used — South African market conditions differ significantly enough that international data would be misleading to students making local decisions.

Final count: 400 career profiles with full South African data.

Decision: All salary and demand data in the platform is South African-sourced. This is enforced as a design criterion — not a preference.
Research Area 4 — Infrastructure and Access Context
Pre-Development · 22 March 2026
Mobile data costs and access inequality
22 March 2026

Researched mobile data pricing and affordability in South Africa. Sources: Alliance for Affordable Internet 2023 Affordability Report, ICASA (Independent Communications Authority of SA) tariff data, MyBroadband pricing comparisons.

Finding: 1GB of mobile data in South Africa costs approximately 2.5% of average monthly household income — above the 2% threshold the Alliance for Affordable Internet defines as affordable. For students in low-income households, sustained internet research is a real financial cost, not an assumed free resource.

Decision: Data saver mode, code splitting of large data files, and lazy-loaded routes became mandatory requirements rather than optional enhancements.
Load shedding and academic impact
22 March 2026

Investigated the documented impact of load shedding on student study time. Sources: Zondo and Mncube (2022) study in International Journal of Learning, Teaching and Educational Research, Eberhard et al. (2014) World Bank report on South African power infrastructure, Eskom published stage data.

Finding: extended power outages directly reduce study hours and homework completion rates. Students reported that not knowing when outages would occur was as disruptive as the outages themselves. The study recommended that support services provide learners with scheduling tools alongside academic resources.

Decision: Load shedding information was added to the platform scope. This was not a feature addition — it was a finding that the problem being solved was larger than originally scoped.
Section 3
03

Phase 2 — Research During Development

Once building started, research became decision-driven. Every time two viable approaches existed for a technical or design problem, both were investigated before committing to one. The research entries below are the ones where that investigation changed the outcome — cases where looking into the alternatives before choosing meant we did not end up in a dead end.

Framework and Architecture
During Development · March 2026
Next.js vs React + Vite for a fully client-side application
27–28 March 2026

After starting with Next.js, the framework's behavior was investigated against the actual requirements of the project. Specifically: whether server-side rendering was needed (it was not — all data is static or fetched client-side), whether API routes added value (they did not — Supabase handled all backend logic), and whether Next.js's deployment model on Vercel created any specific advantages (it did not, since Vite builds deploy identically).

Sources: Next.js documentation, Vite documentation, Vercel deployment guides, benchmark comparisons of cold build times and bundle analysis.

Finding: Next.js adds build complexity and routing conventions that were actively working against the project's structure. Vite's rollupOptions allowed more granular code splitting, which was required to meet the low-data performance criterion.

Decision: Switched to React + Vite on 28 March. Next.js removed entirely.
Redux vs React Context for global state management
25 March 2026

Researched the tradeoffs between Redux Toolkit and React's built-in Context API for managing shared state (auth, user preferences, data saver toggle). Sources: Redux Toolkit documentation, React 19 Context documentation, several comparisons of bundle size impact.

Finding: Redux Toolkit adds approximately 12KB to the gzipped bundle. For an app of Prospect's scale — one global auth state, one preferences context — this overhead is not justified. React Context handles these cases without additional dependencies and without the boilerplate overhead Redux requires.

Decision: React Context used throughout. No Redux dependency added.
Database and Backend
During Development · March 2026
Supabase vs Firebase for authentication and database
25 March 2026

Compared Supabase and Firebase across: database model (PostgreSQL vs Firestore's document model), free tier limits, client SDK complexity, and suitability for structured relational data like career profiles and user progress records.

Finding: the career and bursary data has a relational structure — careers link to subjects, subjects link to APS requirements, bursaries filter by province. PostgreSQL (Supabase) handles this more naturally than Firestore's document model. Supabase's free tier was also more generous for the project's scale. Supabase Auth provided email confirmation, session persistence, and password reset out of the box.

Decision: Supabase adopted for database, authentication, and real-time features.
AI Backend — Three Investigations
During Development · May 2026
Google Gemini API — free tier limits and rate behavior
13 May 2026

Investigated Google Gemini's free tier rate limits before and during integration. Sources: Gemini API documentation, Google AI Studio rate limit documentation.

Finding: Gemini's free tier allows a limited number of requests per day, which resets at midnight UTC. The quota was exhausted during development testing — before the platform was even live to real users. On the live site, the first test returned HTTP 429 (Too Many Requests). There is no mechanism to increase the free tier limit without upgrading to a paid plan.

Decision: Gemini removed. A local predefined answer system was built immediately as a fallback — 7 answers to the most common student questions that return instantly without any API call.
Ollama — local LLM runner viability for a hosted web application
15 May 2026

Investigated Ollama as an AI backend. Ollama runs large language models locally on a machine and exposes them via a local HTTP server. The llama3.2 model (2.0GB) was downloaded and tested, along with the llava model (4.7GB) for image understanding. Both worked well locally — responses were accurate and fast.

The critical research question was: how does Ollama behave in a deployed web application? Investigation of Ollama's architecture confirmed it listens on localhost:11434 by default. A browser-based web app hosted on Vercel cannot route requests to a user's localhost. Every user visiting the site would be attempting to connect to Ollama on their own machine, which would not be running it.

Finding: Ollama is a local development tool. It cannot function as a backend for a cloud-hosted web application without a dedicated server running Ollama and exposed to the internet — which requires infrastructure the project did not have.

Decision: Ollama removed. Confirmed as useful for local development and testing only, not for production deployment.
Groq API — cloud inference speed and free tier viability
15 May 2026

Researched Groq as a cloud inference provider. Sources: Groq developer documentation, independent benchmarks comparing Groq vs OpenAI vs Anthropic on response latency, Groq free tier terms.

Finding: Groq's free tier provides significantly more requests per day than Gemini's, with a daily token limit rather than a per-minute request cap. The llama-3.3-70b-versatile model on Groq returns first tokens in under 2 seconds for most queries. Groq uses an OpenAI-compatible API format, meaning the integration required minimal code changes from the Gemini implementation. The free tier was sufficient for a student-facing platform at current scale.

Decision: Groq adopted as the AI backend. Confirmed working end-to-end on the live URL before launch.
Feature Scope Decisions During Development
During Development · April–May 2026
Community Impact and pothole reporting — audience fit investigation
11 and 13 May 2026

Both features were built before being investigated against the core design criterion: does this directly serve a Grade 8–12 learner's ability to plan their education or career? Community Impact asked students to submit testimonials about how Prospect helped them — not a primary student need. Pothole reporting was civic utility with no connection to education or career decisions.

Both features required their own authenticated submission flows, Supabase tables, and maintenance overhead. The research question was whether this overhead was justified by the value delivered to the target audience.

Finding: neither feature served the core user's primary task. Both added complexity and maintenance burden without returning value proportional to the cost.

Decision: Both features removed. Informed the principle that civic content is only included in the platform when it directly affects a student's ability to study or plan — which load shedding, water availability, exam dates, and cost of living all do.
Auth requirements for informational community pages
15 May 2026

Investigated whether the load shedding tracker, water dashboard, cost of living tool, and civics guide should require a user account to access. The question was: is there any user-specific data on these pages that justifies an auth requirement?

Finding: none of these pages store or display any user-specific data. They are purely informational. Requiring login to view public information creates friction with no purpose — particularly for a student who just wants to check when their next power cut is without creating an account first.

Decision: Auth requirement removed from all informational community pages. Auth is only required for features that store user-specific data: profile, progress tracking, saved items, forums.
Section 4
04

Phase 3 — Research After Development

Once the platform was built, the research focus shifted to verification. The questions changed from "what should we build?" to "does what we built actually work?" and "does it address the problem we identified?" This phase covered functional testing, design criteria auditing, performance measurement, and data completeness verification.

End-to-End Testing
Post-Development · May 2026
Playwright end-to-end test suite — all critical user flows
7–8 May and 15 May 2026

31 automated end-to-end tests were written and run using Playwright against the live development server. Tests covered: user registration and sign-in, session persistence, career browsing and filtering, career quiz completion from start to results, bursary search and filter, bursary detail page navigation, school assist chat (predefined answers and Groq responses), study library navigation, APS calculator input and output, NSC exam countdown display, and load shedding page data rendering.

A test mode URL parameter (?__test_mode=true) was implemented in the authentication system so tests could access auth-gated pages in a CI environment without requiring live Supabase credentials. This was itself a research-driven decision — standard auth testing approaches (mocking Supabase, using test credentials) had failure modes that the URL parameter approach avoided.

Finding: all 31 tests passed. Three tests required fixes after the codebase reorganization on 8 May changed import paths — these were corrected and retested before launch.

Outcome: 31/31 tests passing at launch. Test suite confirms all critical user journeys work end-to-end on the live platform.
Design Criteria Audit
Post-Development · 15 May 2026
Full platform audit against established design criteria
13–15 May 2026

Each of the ten design criteria established before development was audited against the live platform. The audit method for each criterion:

SA specificity: Career salary and demand data cross-checked against source references (PayScale SA, CareerJunction, Stats SA). International data would be flagged and replaced.

Mobile usability: Every page tested at 375px viewport width in Chrome DevTools. Touch target sizes measured using browser inspector. Active page indicator confirmed present in all navigation contexts.

Low-data performance: Vite bundle analyzer run to verify career and bursary data files are split into separate chunks. Network tab checked to confirm lazy-loaded routes do not download until navigated to.

TVET parity: TVET section navigation position checked against university section. Depth of content (college listings, course detail, career links, funding information) compared directly.

AI reliability: Chat tested with and without Groq API available. Predefined answers confirmed returning instantly for all 7 common questions. Timeout behavior tested by simulating a slow connection.

Live data currency: GitHub Actions logs reviewed to confirm scraper workflows ran on schedule. Load shedding and water JSON files verified as updated since the previous day.

Free access: All informational pages confirmed accessible without login. Auth requirement confirmed present only on pages that require a user account to function (profile, progress, forums).

Outcome: All 10 criteria passed at launch. No exceptions or waivers applied.
Data Completeness Verification
Post-Development · 15 May 2026
Bursary database — completeness and accuracy check
15 May 2026

The 245 bursary entries were reviewed against the BURSARY-REVIEW.md audit trail. Each entry was checked for: name, provider, field of study, eligibility criteria (minimum APS or mark, citizenship, financial means test), funding amount or "varies," application deadline or "rolling," province applicability, and application link.

Finding: all 245 entries had all required fields populated. Eleven entries had "varies" for funding amount — these were confirmed cases where the provider genuinely does not publish a fixed figure, not data gaps.

Outcome: 245/245 entries complete. Audit trail maintained in BURSARY-REVIEW.md.
Career database — schema completeness across all 400 profiles
15 May 2026

All 400 career profiles were verified against the defined schema: title, sector, description, required subjects (list), recommended subjects (list), minimum APS score, salary range (SA-sourced, in ZAR), provincial job demand indicator, qualification path, and grade-by-grade preparation notes for Grades 10, 11, and 12. TypeScript type checking was the primary verification method — the TypeScript compiler would fail to build if any profile was missing a required field.

Finding: TypeScript build passed without errors on all 400 profiles. Two duplicate entries had been removed during pre-development data cleaning and did not reappear.

Outcome: 400/400 career profiles complete and type-safe. Build confirms no missing required fields.
Performance Measurement
Post-Development · 15 May 2026
AI response time measurement — Groq vs predefined answers
15 May 2026

Measured response times for both the predefined answer layer and Groq API responses. Method: browser network tab timing for Groq, console.time() measurements for predefined answers. 10 test queries run for each.

Findings: Predefined answers: consistently under 50ms (local memory lookup, no network call). Groq API text responses: average 2.1 seconds from send to first token rendered. Longest observed: 4.8 seconds. All under the 5-second design criterion target. No timeouts observed in 10 test queries.

Outcome: Both response layers meet their design criterion targets. Predefined layer provides instant fallback well under 100ms target.
Scope Verification Against Original Research
Post-Development · 15 May 2026
Platform coverage vs original problem findings
15 May 2026

The five phenomena identified in pre-development research were checked against what the platform actually delivered at launch.

Subject choice disconnect: Addressed by the career explorer with subject filtering, the APS calculator, the career quiz linking subjects to ranked careers, and subject guidance pages explaining NSC groupings.

Fragmented information: Addressed by centralizing 400 careers, 245 bursaries, TVET listings, university information, and study resources in one platform with unified search and navigation.

TVET misrepresentation: Addressed by the full TVET section with navigational parity, college listings, course detail, and career outcome data — presented as a first-choice pathway, not a fallback.

Infrastructure as an educational barrier: Addressed by the load shedding tracker with live Eskom stage data, water availability dashboard, NSC exam countdown, and cost of living information by city.

Financial literacy gap: Addressed by the tax and budget calculator, cost of living data, and bursary listings that explicitly state what is and is not covered by each bursary.

Outcome: All five phenomena identified in pre-development research are addressed by features present in the launched platform. No identified problem was left unaddressed.
